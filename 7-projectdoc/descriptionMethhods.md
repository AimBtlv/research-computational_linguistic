#### Method 3: Collective Memory
The results were immediate and clear. Wikipedia does not forget the way printed books do, and Figure 2 shows exactly how the forgetting pattern differs.
Panel A reveals two distinct behaviours depending on how recent the target year is. Older historical years such as 1914, 1918, 1929, and 1945 appear as flat, low-frequency lines running horizontally across the entire observation window. They are consistently present but never prominent, sitting at roughly 1 to 2 occurrences per 10,000 tokens throughout 2004 to 2024. Recent years behave entirely differently. The year 2011 produced the sharpest peak in the dataset, reaching over 30 occurrences per 10,000 tokens around 2012 to 2013 before declining gradually. The year 2001 peaked early and stabilised rather than disappeared. This contrast between flat old-year lines and peaked recent-year curves is the central finding of this panel, and it has a structural explanation. Articles about World War I or the Great Depression were written years before our observation window began and changed very little after that. Articles about 2011 or 2008 were actively created and expanded in the years immediately following those events, producing the visible peak, and then stabilised as editorial attention moved elsewhere.
Panel B makes the comparison with Michel et al. precise. Every measurable half-life in our corpus falls between 2 and 6 years, far below Michel et al.'s reference values of 32 years for "1880" and 10 years for "1973," shown as dashed lines at the top of the panel. This looks like dramatically faster forgetting. But the mechanism is completely different. In Google Books, a declining half-life means that fewer authors across society chose to write about a year as time passed. In Wikipedia, a half-life of 2 to 6 years means that editors reorganised or rewrote article sections, removing or restructuring the sentences that contained those date references. The formula is identical. What it measures is not.
Panel C adds the most theoretically productive finding of this method. The lag between a historical event and its peak Wikipedia frequency decreases sharply for more recent events. Events from 1914 and 1918 reached their peak Wikipedia discussion 67 to 78 years after they occurred. Events from the 2000s peaked within 1 to 4 years. This directly parallels Michel et al.'s observation that cultural adoption and attention accelerate over time. Here, however, the driver is structural rather than social. Wikipedia simply did not exist before 2001, so earlier events entered the corpus only as retrospective references written by editors who already knew the full historical outcome, producing a very different kind of attention curve from events that unfolded in real time as Wikipedia was being written.
Panel D gives the full picture as a heatmap. The darkest cells, representing the highest mention frequency, cluster around 2001, 2008, and 2011 in the years immediately following those events. Earlier target years like 1914 and 1929 appear only as faint persistent background colour across all observation years. This is exactly the editorial inertia argument made visible: recent events produce bursts of active editorial attention that eventually calm down, while distant events produce a low stable presence that no one particularly maintains but no one removes either.
Taken together, the four panels point to the same conclusion. The collective memory method transfers to Wikipedia but answers a different question. Michel et al. measured how long society chose to remember. In a living document corpus the same method measures how long editors chose not to edit. These are not the same phenomenon, and recognising the difference is the methodological contribution this experiment makes.
